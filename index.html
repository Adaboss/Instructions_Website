Determining a monthly allowance

The first stage of understanding your personal finance is knowing how much money you actually have every month! For some this might be simple, for others you might need to dig deep. It’s okay if you don’t know exactly how much that is now, you can determine the exact amount later with our helpful spreadsheet.

We don’t know the specifics of your situation, so it’s up to you to determine what counts towards your monthly funds. Here are some cases that may apply to you:

  • Monthly allowance from family
  • Work Study or any income from Jobs or side hustles
  • Apply a budget based on funds you might have saved in a checking or savings account
  • Financial Aid Received on a semester basis can be divided by 4 to give you a monthly allowance

50/30/20 Sheet

One of the easiest ways to see if you are living within your means is using the 50/30/20 rule. This means that each month you allocate 50% to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings. This can actually be quite hard to stick to as a college student. As such, we recommend splitting this into 65/25/10 (65% needs, 25% wants, 10% savings).

Remember, a need is something that you need to live. This means rent/housing, groceries/meal plan, utilities, or maybe even car insurance if applicable. A want is something that you can physically live without. So yes, the $8.25 coffee you get doesn’t count as a need. Essentially, any of your monthly expenses that aren’t basic needs, are wants.

Finally, you can count savings as anything that is left over at the end of the month. We will dive further into how you can use your money to save better with special types of savings accounts.

Tracking Your Spending

Tracking your spending helps you understand where your money is going each month.

Why it helps

Many people underestimate how much they spend on small purchases like food, coffee, or subscriptions.

Ways to track spending

  • Using a budgeting spreadsheet
  • Using mobile budgeting apps
  • Checking bank statements regularly

Be consistent

Updating your spending weekly or monthly will help you stay on track with your budget.

Debt Snowball vs. Debt Avalanche

There are two popular strategies to pay off debt while continuing to make the minimum payments on everything:

  • Debt Snowball (Focus on Morale): Put any extra money toward the debt with the smallest balance first. This gives you quick wins by eliminating individual debts faster, building momentum to stay motivated!
  • Debt Avalanche (Focus on Math): Put extra money toward the debt with the highest interest rate first. This saves you the most money on interest overall, but it can take significantly longer before you see an individual debt wiped out.

Credit Card Usage

Credit cards can be helpful if used responsibly. They can help you build credit, which is crucial for renting apartments, buying a car, or getting loans later in life. Additionally, credit cards can give you rewards that can actually make you money just by buying the things you were already paying for! Here are some tips to stay aware of your credit card usage:

Tip 1 – Only spend what you can pay back

Treat your credit card like a debit card. Only spend money you already have.

Tip 2 – Pay your balance every month

Pay the full balance each month to avoid interestThe cost of borrowing money, or the money earned on savings, typically expressed as a percentage. charges.

Tip 3 – Keep your credit usage low

Keep your credit usage below 30% of your credit limit. This means if you have a credit limit of $1,000, you should keep your balance below $300 every month.

Tip 4 – Avoid unnecessary debt

Credit cards should be used carefully as they carry high interest rates as shown above in the debt snowball calculator. Avoid using them for things you cannot afford.

Short-Term Goals vs Long-Term Goals

Understanding what is important to you financially can help you make better spending decisions.

Short-Term Goals

These are things you want to achieve soon, usually within the next year.

  • Saving for a trip
  • Buying a laptop
  • Building a small emergency fund

Long-Term Goals

These goals take several years to achieve.

  • Paying off student loans
  • Saving for a house
  • Investing for retirement

Why this matters

Knowing your goals helps you decide where your money should go each month.

The Importance of Investing

Investing allows your money to grow over time instead of just sitting in a bank account.

Why investing matters

When you invest, your money can grow through compound interest and market returns.

Start early

Even small amounts invested regularly can grow significantly over many years.

HYSA and Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Different types of financial accounts can help your money grow faster or reduce taxes.

High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)

A HYSA offers a higher interest rate than a regular savings account, helping your savings grow faster while still being easy to access.

Tax-Advantaged Accounts

These accounts offer tax benefits and are commonly used for long-term savings.

  • Roth IRAAn individual retirement account (IRA) offering tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement, funded with after-tax dollars.
  • 401(k)An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan that lets employees save and invest a piece of their paycheck before taxes are taken out.
  • Health Savings Accounts (HSA)A tax-advantaged account for people with a high-deductible health plan, used to save and pay for qualified medical expenses tax-free.

Why they matter

Using the right accounts can help you save more money over time and reduce taxes.
